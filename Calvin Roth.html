1 The Quantum Mechanics foundations

In this post, I hope to explain the neccessary physics background the governs quantum computing. Ideally, this will be a useful layer of abstraction from physics of quantum mechanics into something that is more practical. I will add onto this post as I see fit. There are four main postulates of quantum mechanics that I feel provide a useful starting point for how to think about quantum mechanics. In addition to introducing these postulates I will discuss some related ideas and background information.

1.1 How States are represented

 Postulate 1 Postulate 1: An isolated physical system can be fully described by an element of a finite dimmensional complex vector space with the following properties:

  1. The space has an inner product
  2. Every element representing a physical state has length 1

A complex vector space with an inner product defined on it is called a Hilbert Space. The fact that we can model a physical systems with a vector space is not surprising to me as the idea we can describe an object's behavior by some of it's values such as weight, position, temperature, etc. Furthermore, we are not limiting what these vectors are allowed to look like . The second point that every element has length 1 isn't intuitive at first glance though. But for now I would like to withhold on why this is the case.

Notice this postulate is especially modest because it doesn't describe what these states need to look like or how to determine them instead just that the system can be modelled via Hillbert Spaces.

1.1.1 Bra-Ket notation

Although it is possible talk about qubits with vector notation almost every quantum scientist uses bra-ket notation which is a way to represent vectors in a way that is more convenient for the commonly used operations. A ket is what what we use to signify the state of qubit. We write a ket like |ϕ⟩|ϕ⟩ where phi is the state we are trying to represent. In common notation we let |0⟩=[1,0]t|0⟩=[1,0]t and |1⟩=[0,1]t|1⟩=[0,1]t and can express any other 2 dimmensional vector as a linear combination of |0⟩ and |1⟩. The kets |0⟩ and |1⟩ are called the computational basis. A bra ⟨ϕ|⟨ϕ| is simply the complex conjugate transpose of the ket |ϕ⟩|ϕ⟩. Computer Scientists and mathematicains would say a qubit is in state $|φ ⟩ = a | 0 ⟩ + b | 1 ⟩ $ which is a linear combination of |0⟩,|1⟩|0⟩,|1⟩ but a physicist would say the qubit is in a superposition of |0⟩ and |1⟩. Borrowing from physicists, we call the magnitude of a and b amplitudes. This notation is practical becuase in quantum computing we will frequently want to do inner products which are usually denoted by $⟨

⋅ , ⋅ ⟩$ and outer products. The inner product between states |a⟩|a⟩ and |b⟩|b⟩ is ⟨a|b⟩⟨a|b⟩ which looks like inner product notation. The outer product in standard mathematics between vectors u and v is u×v=W,wij=uivju×v=W,wij=uivj or as u∗vtu∗vt. In bra ket notation, the outer product would be |a⟩⟨b||a⟩⟨b| which the vertical bars on the side resemble a matrix.

1.2.1 What Do We Obervers 'See'

People in the quantum community frequently talk about the current state of the qubit during computation in a way that can be misleading if you are only familar with classical mechanics. They might say the state is currently 0.8|0⟩+0.6|1⟩0.8|0⟩+0.6|1⟩ before a changed is applied and then after it is in the state 0.6|0⟩+0.8|1⟩0.6|0⟩+0.8|1⟩. The thing to bear in mind is that although one can talk about how a physical object affects the qubits and even write down the unitary matrix that represents this change, we fundementally can not actually measure the state of the qubit without fundementally distrubing it and is known as measurement. This is different than a classical bit where we can check if the bit is 0 or 1 then continue without distrubing the system. So although it appears like we know the amplitudes of |0⟩|0⟩ and |1⟩|1⟩ to some infinite precision we can't actually 'see' the qubit at any point in the process.

2.2 What are the Essential Single Qubit Gates

In line with classical logic gates, we have quantum gates to take qubits as input and transoform them somehow. This gates are via the rules described above unitary matrices. What do the most commonly used gates look like. Technically, the identity matrix is the obvious starting point since we will very frequently want to not do something to specific qubits during computation. Here is an example of how we might generate a circuit drawing with python. 

qc = QuantumCircuit(3,3)
qc.x(1)
qc.measure(0,0)
qI = qc.draw("mpl")
# Use .show or .savefig(fname) to actually generate images

In this code, we create a circuit with 3 inputs and three ouputs where the 1st(0 indexed) qubit is measured and told to write a 0 on the image to the screen, the 2nd has a gate that we will see soon call the X-gate applied to it, and the third has no action on it.

But that's a bit boring hopefully we can do more. The first trio of gates are known as the Pauli Gates or the X,Y,Z gates. The X gate reprented by the matrix [0110][0110]. Applying to a qubit a|0⟩+b|1⟩a|0⟩+b|1⟩ we see that it swaps the a and b values [0110][ab]=[ba][0110][ab]=[ba]

The Y gate is [0i−i0][0−ii0] and the Z is [100−1][100−1]. Reader can quickly verify that Z|0⟩=|0⟩Z|0⟩=|0⟩ and Z|1⟩=−|1⟩Z|1⟩=−|1⟩. The reason why is that as vectors |0⟩,|1⟩|0⟩,|1⟩ are the eigenvectors of the matrix. For this reason the computational basis is sometimes called the Z-basis. Taking this as a hint for what to do next we consider the eigenvectors of the X basis.

This eigenvectors are called the X-basis or the hadamard basis are the next most important basis to know for quantum computing. Notice that these vectors when viewed geoemetrically are not random but instead are vectors half way between |0⟩ and |1⟩ IMAGE

2.2.1 Probabability example

Suppose we are given the |+⟩|+⟩ ket. What is the probablity that when measured in the Z-basis the measurement will be a |0⟩|0⟩? A |1⟩|1⟩?

As previously discussed the measurement operator for |0⟩|0⟩ is |0⟩⟨0|=M0|0⟩⟨0|=M0 so the probability of measuring a 0 is

⟨+|Mt0M0|+⟩=⟨+|M0|+⟩=[12–√,0]|+⟩=0.5(6)(7)(8)(6)⟨+|M0tM0|+⟩=⟨+|M0|+⟩(7)=[12,0]|+⟩(8)=0.5

Either by observing the total probabability should sum to one we see that the chance of measuring a |1⟩|1⟩ is also 0.5. An good exercise is to verify that that the same holds for measuring a |−⟩|−⟩ in the computational basis. We can also check that our math is correct by using Qiskit to simulate this circuit

A view that will be useful to keep in mind for future developments is that |+⟩,|−⟩|+⟩,|−⟩ represent states that are maximally mixed up from the Z-basis.

The Hadamard gate H is the next gate we will introduce. It is fact just the vectors of the X-basis

2.3 What are the Essential Multi Qubit Gates

Extending ourselves to acting on more than one qubit we have many more options to consider for different gates which again are represented by matrices but now are of size 2n×2n2n×2n where n is the number of qubits. The easiest class of multi qubit gates are those that are single qubit gates acting in parallel on different qubits. Given gates U1,U2U1,U2 and qubits |ϕ⟩,|ϕ′⟩|ϕ⟩,|ϕ′⟩ we can write 2 qubit gates formed by single qubits as Utotal|ϕtotal=U1ϕ⟩⊗U2ϕ′Utotal|ϕtotal=U1ϕ⟩⊗U2ϕ′. 

qc = QuantumCircuit(2)
qc.x(0)
qc.y(1)

We should pause for a second and consider that right now it isn't entirely clearly with matrix multiplication of unitary matrix with vectors is sufficient to realize classical algorithms let alone surprass classical methods. So maybe we should develop gates that will help us emulate classical gates. In particular, if we could emulate the NAND gate we would be done as that gate is known to be turing complete.

Let's try and derive what a commonly used matrix, called the controlled not or CNOT, should look like. The controlled not takes in 2 qubits which are labelled the control target. This gate is supposed to have the effect that is the control is |1⟩|1⟩ then we apply an X gate(the quantum equivalent to not) to the target qubit otherwise do nothing. So we should have the following table of inputs and outputs

Control in Target in Control out Target out
0 0 0 0
0 1 0 1
1 0 1 1
1 1 1 0

So assuming the control is the first qubit and the target is the second we have that

CNOT|00⟩=CNOT⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢1000⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥=⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢1000⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥,CNOT|10⟩=CNOT⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢0010⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥=⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢0001⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥,CNOT|01⟩=CNOT⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢0100⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥=⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢0100⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥,CNOT|11⟩=CNOT⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢0001⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥=⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢0010⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥CNOT|00⟩=CNOT[1000]=[1000],CNOT|01⟩=CNOT[0100]=[0100],CNOT|10⟩=CNOT[0010]=[0001],CNOT|11⟩=CNOT[0001]=[0010]

So the operations seem to indicate that the first two dimmensions of the 2-qubit system remain fixed and the bottom two examples indicate that the final two two dimmensions get swapped as in ⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢⋅⋅10⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥→⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢⋅⋅01⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥[⋅⋅10]→[⋅⋅01] and vice versea. Therefore we conclude that are matrix should hopefully look like ⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢1000010000010010⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥[1000010000010010]

This is in fact what the gate looks like but it is a bit misleading in that it looks like the first qubit never changes. Consider

CNOT|+0⟩=⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢1000010000010010⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢12√012√0⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥=⎡⎣⎢⎢⎢⎢⎢12√0012√⎤⎦⎥⎥⎥⎥⎥=12–√(|00⟩+|11⟩)(9)(10)(11)(9)CNOT|+0⟩=[1000010000010010][120120](10)=[120012](11)=12(|00⟩+|11⟩)

This final expression is what is known as an entangled state. An exercise is to verify the probabilities of measuring 00 and 11 are 50% each and the probability of measuring 01 and 10 are 0%. This is interesting because this qubits could be light years away from each other and yet if someone measuring only the first qubit they immidiately know what the second qubit will read then it is measureed. Phsysicists have long be disgruntled at how unintuitive this is. Information can only travel at the speed of light and yet the first measurer can predict the outcome of the second probablistic experiment of the second observer even though light hasn't had time to spread the information. So it appears that information is being spread faster than the speed of light since the state of the second persons qubit changes after the first person measures as it goes from a 50/50 chance of measuring a 0 or 1 two a 100% chance of measuring the same state A measured. Physicists are right in that this is weird but we can take some comfort via the fact the observers can't know the situation faster than the speed of light. For observing A to tell observer B something, she would have to send infomation to B classically, i.e. bounded by the speed of light. So person B doesn't know the state of the qubit has changed and if we repeated this experiment over and over person B would find there is a 50% of measuring 0 or 1, it's just the outcome was determined by what A did.

The final gate we will cover in this section is called the Toffoli gate and it's is basically the extension of the CNOT gate. The Toffoli gate takes two qubits as controls and if both controls are |1⟩|1⟩ then the third target qubit is notted. So it expresses the map |x,y,z⟩→|x,y,z⊕(x∧y)⟩|x,y,z⟩→|x,y,z⊕(x∧y)⟩ where ⊕⊕ is addition modulo 2. From this gate we can construct the NAND gate by setting z to be initial |1⟩|1⟩.
